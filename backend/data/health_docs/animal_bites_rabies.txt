ANIMAL BITES AND RABIES PREVENTION FOR RURAL HEALTHCARE

Rabies is almost always fatal once symptoms begin, but it is 100% preventable with correct wound washing and timely vaccination. Any bite, scratch, or lick on broken skin from a dog, cat, monkey, jackal, mongoose, or bat must be treated seriously.

1. DOG, CAT, MONKEY OR OTHER ANIMAL BITE (RABIES RISK)
Immediate First Aid (very important): Wash the wound immediately with running water and soap for at least 15 minutes. This alone greatly lowers the risk of rabies. Then apply an antiseptic such as Povidone-Iodine. Do NOT apply chilli powder, lime, oil, turmeric, herbs, or cow dung — these do not help and can cause infection. Do NOT cover the wound tightly or stitch it immediately.
What to Do Next: Go to the nearest PHC or hospital the same day. Anti-Rabies Vaccine (ARV) is needed for almost all bites, and Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG) may be needed for deep or severe bites. These are given free at government hospitals. Complete the full vaccine schedule on the correct days — do not skip doses.
Safe Medicine: Paracetamol for pain. Tetanus injection is also needed.
Red-Flag / Emergency: Bites on the face, neck, hands, or genitals; deep or multiple wounds; bites from a stray or wild animal; or any bite from an animal that later dies or behaves strangely — seek urgent medical care immediately.
Warning Signs of Rabies (medical emergency): Fear of water, fear of air or light, difficulty swallowing, excessive saliva, confusion, or unusual behaviour after a past bite — go to hospital at once.

2. SNAKE BITE (see also emergency guidance)
First Aid: Keep the person calm and still; movement spreads venom. Keep the bitten limb below heart level and immobile, like a fracture. Remove rings, bangles, and tight clothing near the bite. Do NOT cut the wound, suck the venom, apply a tight tourniquet, or use traditional remedies.
What to Do Next: Take the person to a PHC or hospital with anti-snake venom IMMEDIATELY. Note the time of the bite and the snake's appearance if seen safely.
Emergency: Difficulty breathing, drooping eyelids, drowsiness, bleeding, or swelling spreading up the limb — this is a life-threatening emergency.

3. INSECT AND BEE/WASP STINGS
First Aid: Remove the sting if visible by scraping sideways (do not squeeze). Wash with soap and water and apply a cold compress to reduce swelling.
Safe Medicine: An antihistamine such as Cetirizine can reduce itching and swelling. Paracetamol for pain.
Red-Flag / Emergency: Many stings at once, or any sign of a severe allergic reaction — swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat, difficulty breathing, dizziness, or fainting — is an emergency; go to hospital immediately.

4. SCORPION STING
First Aid: Keep the person calm and the part still. Wash the area and apply a cold compress. Give Paracetamol for pain.
What to Do Next: Visit the PHC, especially for children, as severe stings can need treatment.
Emergency: Difficulty breathing, drooling, vomiting, sweating, or restlessness, especially in a child — seek urgent care.

PREVENTION SUMMARY: Vaccinate pet dogs against rabies. Avoid touching stray or wild animals. Teach children not to disturb animals while eating or sleeping. Wear footwear and use a torch when walking at night to avoid snakes and scorpions. Keep surroundings clean to reduce stray animals and pests.
