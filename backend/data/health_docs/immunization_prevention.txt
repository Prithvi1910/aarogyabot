IMMUNIZATION AND DISEASE PREVENTION GUIDANCE FOR RURAL HEALTHCARE

Prevention is cheaper and safer than treatment. Vaccines, clean water, hygiene, and a balanced diet prevent most serious childhood and infectious diseases. Government immunization is free at PHCs, sub-centres, and Anganwadis.

1. CHILDHOOD IMMUNIZATION (UNIVERSAL IMMUNIZATION PROGRAMME)
Why It Matters: Vaccines protect children from tuberculosis, polio, diphtheria, whooping cough, tetanus, hepatitis B, measles, rubella, pneumonia, diarrhoea (rotavirus), and more.
Key Schedule (free at government centres):
- At birth: BCG (for TB), OPV-0 (polio), Hepatitis B birth dose.
- 6, 10, and 14 weeks: Pentavalent (DPT + Hepatitis B + Hib), OPV, Rotavirus, and PCV (pneumococcal) doses.
- 9-12 months: Measles-Rubella (MR) first dose, and Vitamin A first dose.
- 16-24 months: MR second dose, DPT booster, OPV booster.
- 5-6 years: DPT booster.
- 10 and 16 years: Td (tetanus-diphtheria).
Advice: Keep the child's immunization card safe, attend every due visit, and complete all doses. Mild fever or soreness after a vaccine is normal — give fluids and Paracetamol if needed.

2. TETANUS PROTECTION
Why It Matters: Tetanus enters through wounds and is often fatal but fully preventable.
Advice: Pregnant women receive Td/tetanus injections during antenatal care. After a deep or dirty wound or animal bite, get a tetanus injection at the PHC.

3. ANTENATAL (PREGNANCY) CARE
Why It Matters: Regular check-ups protect both mother and baby.
Advice: Register the pregnancy early and attend at least four antenatal check-ups. Take Iron-Folic Acid and calcium tablets, get Td injections, and plan a delivery at a health facility. Watch for danger signs: severe headache, blurred vision, swelling of face/hands, severe vomiting, bleeding, reduced baby movement, or fits — seek care immediately.

4. SAFE WATER AND SANITATION
Why It Matters: Prevents diarrhoea, cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A, and worms.
Advice: Drink safe water (boiled, filtered, or chlorinated). Store water in a clean, covered container and take it out with a ladle. Use a toilet and never defecate in the open. Keep food covered and eat it freshly cooked.

5. HAND AND PERSONAL HYGIENE
Advice: Wash hands with soap before cooking and eating and after using the toilet or handling animals. Keep nails short. Bathe regularly and wear clean clothes. Cover the mouth with the elbow when coughing or sneezing.

6. VECTOR (MOSQUITO) CONTROL
Why It Matters: Prevents malaria, dengue, chikungunya, and Japanese encephalitis.
Advice: Remove stagnant water from pots, tyres, coolers, and drains weekly. Sleep under insecticide-treated mosquito nets. Use screens and repellents. Wear full-sleeved clothes at dawn and dusk.

7. SEASONAL DISEASE PREVENTION
Monsoon: Higher risk of dengue, malaria, leptospirosis, typhoid, and diarrhoea — protect against mosquitoes, avoid wading in dirty water, and drink safe water.
Summer: Risk of heat stroke and dehydration — drink plenty of fluids, avoid midday sun, and use ORS.
Winter: Risk of colds, flu, and pneumonia — keep warm, especially the elderly and young children.

8. LIFESTYLE PREVENTION OF NON-COMMUNICABLE DISEASES
Why It Matters: Prevents diabetes, high blood pressure, heart disease, and cancers.
Advice: Avoid tobacco (smoking and chewing) and limit alcohol. Eat less salt, sugar, and oily food; eat more vegetables and fruits. Stay physically active. Get blood pressure and blood sugar checked at the PHC, especially after age 30.

PREVENTION SUMMARY: Complete all vaccinations, drink safe water, wash hands with soap, control mosquitoes, eat a balanced diet, avoid tobacco and alcohol, and attend regular PHC check-ups for mother, child, and adults.
