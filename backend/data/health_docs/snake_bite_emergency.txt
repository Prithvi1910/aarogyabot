First Aid for Bites and Environmental Emergencies

Snake Bite First Aid
A snake bite is a medical emergency that requires immediate care.
Do's:
1. Keep the victim calm and reassure them. Restricting movement slows venom spread.
2. Keep the bitten limb immobilized at or below heart level.
3. Remove tight clothing, rings, or shoes near the bite before swelling starts.
4. Clean the wound gently with soap and water.
5. Transport the victim immediately to the nearest hospital or PHC that stocks Anti-Snake Venom (ASV).
Don'ts:
1. Do NOT apply a tight tourniquet, as it can restrict blood flow and cause tissue damage.
2. Do NOT cut the wound or try to suck out the venom.
3. Do NOT apply ice, electricity, or herbal remedies.

Dog Bite Protocol and Rabies Prevention
Dog bites carry a high risk of rabies, a fatal viral disease.
1. Wash the wound immediately under running tap water with soap for at least 10-15 minutes. This is critical to wash away the virus.
2. Apply an antiseptic solution if available.
3. Do not cover or bandage the wound unless bleeding is severe.
4. Go to a hospital immediately to receive the Rabies Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) vaccination schedule (Day 0, 3, 7, 14, and 28). If the bite is severe, Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG) may also be required.

Heat-Related Emergencies
It is vital to distinguish between Heat Exhaustion and Heat Stroke.
- Heat Exhaustion: Symptoms include heavy sweating, pale skin, muscle cramps, fatigue, dizziness, headache, and nausea. First Aid: Move to a cool place, loosen clothing, apply cool wet cloths, and sip cool water.
- Heat Stroke (Life-Threatening): Symptoms include high body temperature (above 103°F/39.4°C), red, hot, dry skin (no sweating), rapid pulse, headache, confusion, or unconsciousness. First Aid: Call for emergency help immediately. Cool the person down by immersion in cool water or placing ice packs on the neck, armpits, and groin.
