BASIC FIRST AID FOR COMMON EMERGENCIES IN RURAL AREAS

Knowing simple first aid can save a life in the minutes before reaching a Primary Health Centre (PHC) or hospital. Stay calm, keep the person safe, and arrange transport early for any serious case.

1. BURNS (FIRE, HOT WATER, OIL, STEAM)
First Aid: Move the person away from the heat source. Cool the burnt area under clean, cool (not ice-cold) running water for 15-20 minutes. Remove rings and tight items before swelling starts. Cover loosely with a clean, non-fluffy cloth. Do NOT apply toothpaste, oil, ghee, ink, mud, or butter. Do NOT break blisters.
Safe Medicine: Paracetamol for pain.
Red-Flag / Emergency: Burns larger than the person's palm, burns on the face, hands, feet, joints, or genitals, deep or charred burns, electrical or chemical burns, or burns in a child or elderly person — go to hospital urgently.

2. CHOKING (FOOD OR OBJECT STUCK IN THROAT)
If the person can cough or speak: Encourage them to keep coughing.
If they cannot breathe, cough, or speak: Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand. If not cleared, give abdominal thrusts (stand behind, press inward and upward above the navel) until the object comes out. For a baby, use back blows and chest thrusts, not abdominal thrusts.
Emergency: If the person becomes unconscious, call for help and start chest compressions; rush to hospital.

3. CUTS WITH HEAVY BLEEDING
First Aid: Press firmly on the wound with a clean cloth and keep pressing without lifting to check. Raise the injured part above the level of the heart if possible. Add more cloth on top if blood soaks through; do not remove the first layer.
Emergency: Bleeding that does not stop after 10 minutes of firm pressure, spurting blood, or a deep wound — go to hospital urgently. A tetanus injection is needed for deep or dirty wounds.

4. FRACTURES AND SPRAINS
First Aid: Do not move the injured part unnecessarily. Support a suspected broken bone with a splint (a straight stick and cloth) to stop movement. For a sprain, remember RICE: Rest, Ice (cold compress), Compression (firm bandage), Elevation. Apply cold, not heat, in the first 1-2 days.
Safe Medicine: Paracetamol for pain.
Red-Flag / Emergency: Visible deformity, bone through the skin, inability to move or bear weight, numbness, or a cold/blue limb — go to hospital.

5. HEAT STROKE AND HEAT EXHAUSTION
Most Common Cause: Working or walking long in hot sun without enough water.
Signs: Heat exhaustion — heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, muscle cramps. Heat stroke (dangerous) — very hot dry skin, no sweating, high body temperature, confusion, or unconsciousness.
First Aid: Move to a cool, shaded place. Loosen clothing. Cool the body with wet cloths and fanning. If conscious, give ORS or water with a little salt and sugar to sip.
Emergency: Confusion, unconsciousness, fits, or hot dry skin is heat stroke — a medical emergency; cool the body and rush to hospital.

6. FAINTING
First Aid: Lay the person flat and raise their legs slightly. Loosen tight clothing and ensure fresh air. Once awake, let them rest and sip water.
Red-Flag / Emergency: Does not wake within 1-2 minutes, has chest pain, difficulty breathing, fits, or repeated fainting — seek urgent care.

7. ELECTRIC SHOCK
First Aid: Switch off the power source first; do NOT touch the person while they are in contact with electricity. Use a dry wooden stick to separate them if needed. Check breathing.
Emergency: If not breathing, start chest compressions and rush to hospital. All electric burns need medical check.

8. POISONING (SWALLOWED CHEMICAL, PESTICIDE, OR MEDICINE)
First Aid: Do NOT force vomiting unless told by a doctor. Keep any container or label to show the doctor. Keep the person calm.
Emergency: Pesticide poisoning is very dangerous and common in farming areas — take the person and the poison container to hospital immediately.

PREVENTION SUMMARY: Keep a basic first-aid kit (clean cloth/bandage, antiseptic, ORS, Paracetamol, scissors). Store pesticides, kerosene, and medicines locked away from children. Know the route and transport to your nearest PHC and the ambulance number 108.
